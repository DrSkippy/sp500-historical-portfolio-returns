Buy & Hold

The passive baseline — buy once, hold for the full period, sell at the end

Buy & Hold 1 variant  ·  0 parameters  ·  Baseline strategy

Invest everything on day one. Do nothing. Sell on the last day. Buy & Hold is the simplest possible equity strategy and serves as the benchmark against which all active strategies are measured.

How It Works

On the first trading day of the holding period the entire portfolio is converted into S&P 500 shares at the prevailing price. Those shares are held without any adjustment until the last trading day, when they are all sold at the closing price.

📅

Day 1

Buy capital ÷ price shares. Cash balance drops to zero.

⏳

Holding period

No trades. The simulation skips ahead to just before the end date. Cash earns nothing.

🏁

Last day

Sell all shares. Final capital = shares × closing price. Return is calculated.

Return calculation

total_return    = (final_capital − initial_capital) / initial_capital
yearly_return   = (1 + total_return) ^ (1 / years) − 1

Motivation

Buy & Hold is justified by a large body of empirical evidence:

  • Markets trend upward over long horizons. The S&P 500 has delivered positive real returns over every rolling 20-year window in its history. Staying invested captures that drift.
  • Market timing is reliably destructive. Missing the ten best trading days in any decade typically halves long-run returns. Buy & Hold avoids the timing problem entirely by never being out of the market.
  • Transaction costs and taxes are minimised. Every trade has a spread, and in taxable accounts every sale triggers a realisation. With zero intermediate trades, Buy & Hold pays these costs only once.
  • Behavioural advantages. An investor who never checks prices has no opportunity to panic-sell at a bottom or chase a top. Simplicity is a form of discipline.

Within this backtest, Buy & Hold also has a structural advantage: the S&P 500 is a market-cap-weighted index that is itself rebalanced by its index committee, so the investor is passively tracking a continuously updated diversified basket without paying for that maintenance.

Parameters

Buy & Hold has no free parameters. It is a single deterministic strategy. Its performance at any holding period depends entirely on the starting price and the ending price — there are no knobs to tune.

This makes it the ideal baseline: any strategy that adds complexity (and therefore risk of mis-configuration) must justify its added parameters by consistently improving on the Buy & Hold result across the full distribution of outcomes, not just the mean.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Zero trading complexity — impossible to mis-execute
  • Captures full market upside with no drag from rebalancing friction
  • Low sequence-of-returns sensitivity in the middle of the holding period (the entry and exit prices are all that matter)
  • Strong long-term median returns; fraction of losing starts approaches zero beyond 10 years

Limitations

  • Full downside exposure at all times — no volatility dampening
  • At short horizons (1–3 years), losing-start rates near 25–30%
  • No mechanism to add alpha above the market return
  • Cash earns nothing; idle liquidity is a permanent drag compared to mixed-asset strategies

Role in This Backtest

Every chart in the report overlays Buy & Hold as a thick reference line. When evaluating any other strategy, ask two questions:

  1. Does it have a materially lower fraction of losing starts at the horizons you care about?
  2. Does it achieve a higher mean annualised return without an offsetting increase in standard deviation?

If the answer to both is no, the complexity of that strategy is not paying off for the typical investor.
