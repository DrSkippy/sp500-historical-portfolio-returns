Insurance

Equity portfolio with a crash-triggered reserve — absorb sharp drawdowns without selling stocks

← Back to Report Buy & Hold Fractional Kelly Insurance Recent Returns ↗
Insurance 6 variants  ·  2 parameters  ·  ins_frac ∈ {5%, 10%}  ×  deductible ∈ {9%, 12%, 18%}

Hold nearly all capital in equities, but set aside a small reserve that acts as crash insurance. When the market drops sharply over a 6-day window and exceeds a minimum loss threshold (the deductible), the reserve pays out a leveraged multiple of the loss, partially offsetting the equity decline. The reserve earns a small yield otherwise, while the portfolio rebalances every 90 days.

Motivation

The largest single threat to a long-term equity investor is not gradual underperformance — it is a catastrophic drawdown that forces a behavioural exit at the bottom. The 2000–02 dot-com bust, 2008–09 financial crisis, and 2020 pandemic crash each saw the S&P 500 fall 30–55%. Investors who sold during those drawdowns locked in losses and often missed the recovery.

Traditional diversification (bonds, gold) reduces drawdown severity but also permanently caps upside. The Insurance strategy attempts a different trade-off:

  • Keep almost all capital in stocks to maintain equity upside during normal markets.
  • Reserve a small fraction (5–10%) as a "policy" that activates only when a sharp crash occurs.
  • Pay a small ongoing cost (the reserve earns below-market returns) in exchange for a leveraged payout precisely when equity losses are largest.

The goal is to reduce the fraction of losing starts at medium horizons without materially sacrificing the long-run return available from full equity exposure.

How It Works

📅

Day 1

Buy (1 − ins_frac) × capital / price shares. The remaining ins_frac × capital is held as the insurance reserve.

📉

Every trading day

Compare today's price to the price 6 trading days ago. If the 6-day loss ≥ deductible, the payout clause activates.

💰

Payout event

Reserve is replaced by reserve × |loss_fraction| × 10. Portfolio is then rebalanced. The price history window resets.

🔄

Every 90 days

Whether or not a payout occurred, rebalance the portfolio back to the target stock / reserve split.

Payout trigger and size

loss_frac = (price_today − price_6_days_ago) / price_6_days_ago

if loss_frac ≤ −deductible:
    reserve = reserve × |loss_frac| × payout_factor   # payout_factor = 10
    # then rebalance immediately
    price_history.reset()

Example: if deductible = 0.12 and the market falls 15% in 6 days, the reserve grows by 15% × 10 = 150% of its current value. A $500 reserve becomes $1,250 — partially offsetting the $1,500 equity loss on a $10,000 portfolio.

Reserve yield (between payouts)

reserve ×= (1 + insurance_rate_yield) ^ (days_elapsed / 365)
# insurance_rate_yield ≈ 0.5% / year (fixed)

The reserve earns a flat 0.5%/year — substantially below Treasury rates — reflecting the cost of maintaining a liquid crash-response facility. This is the ongoing "insurance premium" the strategy pays.

Inheritance from Fractional Kelly

InsuranceModel extends KellyModel in the codebase. The rebalance routine is identical: after a payout or at each 90-day interval, the total portfolio value (reserve + stock value) is computed and shares are bought or sold to restore the target stock_frac = 1 − ins_frac split. The key difference is that the reserve earns 0.5%/yr instead of the prevailing Treasury rate, and the reserve can jump discontinuously when a payout is triggered.

Parameters Explored

Parameter Values tested What it controls
ins_frac 0.05, 0.10 Fraction of portfolio held as insurance reserve. A larger reserve means a larger payout when triggered, but also a larger permanent drag from the below-market 0.5%/yr yield. At 5% the drag is minimal; at 10% it becomes meaningful over a 10-year horizon.
deductible 0.09, 0.12, 0.18 Minimum 6-day price decline needed to trigger a payout. A lower deductible means the policy fires more often — providing more frequent protection but also resetting the price history window more often, which can fragment coverage. A higher deductible reserves the payout for true crash events only.
rebalance_period 90 days (fixed for all Insurance variants) All Insurance variants rebalance quarterly. This is fixed to isolate the effect of the ins_frac and deductible parameters.
payout_factor 10× (fixed) The leverage multiplier applied to the reserve on a payout event. Fixed at 10 for all variants. Combined with ins_frac = 5–10%, this produces payouts of roughly 0.5–1× the loss on the equity book when triggered.

The 2 × 3 = 6 variants span a range from Ins 5% ded9% (small reserve, fires on moderate crashes) to Ins 10% ded18% (larger reserve, fires only on severe crashes).

Deductible intuition

Thinking of the 6-day window: a 9% decline in 6 trading days corresponds to roughly a 45% annualised crash rate — an extreme event. A 18% decline in 6 days would be historically rare even in the worst market panics. The three deductible values bracket different regimes:

  • 9%: Fires during large corrections (a 2018-style Q4 sell-off might trigger this). More frequent payouts, but each payout is smaller (loss_frac is closer to the deductible floor).
  • 12%: Targets genuine crashes — 2008-style events. Fewer triggers but each with a more substantial loss_frac multiplier.
  • 18%: Only the most severe crashes (early pandemic March 2020, October 1987). Very infrequent; the reserve sits idle most of the time and the ongoing yield drag may dominate over long periods.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Maintains near-full equity exposure during normal markets — captures most of the equity risk premium
  • Crash payouts are countercyclical: the reserve grows precisely when equity losses are largest, providing capital at the best rebalancing opportunity
  • No bond allocation eating into equity upside during bull runs
  • The leveraged payout (10×) means a small reserve fraction produces a meaningful cushion on large drawdown events

Limitations

  • The reserve earns only 0.5%/yr — substantially below risk-free rates. Over 10–15 years on a 10% reserve, this is a ~1–2% total return drag
  • Slow-moving bear markets (gradual 30% decline over 18 months) may never trigger the 6-day threshold, providing no protection
  • After a payout the history window resets, creating a coverage gap immediately following the crash
  • Payout sizing is mechanical — the reserve can be depleted and not immediately restored to target size if a payout produces a large jump
  • 6 variants is a narrower exploration space than Kelly; the optimal parameterisation is likely sensitive to the specific crash profile of the historical period

What the Data Shows

In the backtest results, Insurance variants typically:

  • Sit between Buy & Hold and Fractional Kelly on the risk/return scatter at most horizons — less bond drag than Kelly, slightly more protection than pure Buy & Hold
  • Show the most differentiation from Buy & Hold in periods immediately following a sharp crash — the post-crash rebalance (with an inflated reserve) buys equity at depressed prices
  • Generally underperform the best Kelly variants at 10–15 year horizons because the 0.5%/yr reserve yield is a persistent drag, while Kelly's bond allocation earns the actual Treasury rate
  • Smaller deductibles (9%) tend to produce more consistent (if smaller) payouts; larger deductibles (18%) concentrate protection on rarer, larger events
