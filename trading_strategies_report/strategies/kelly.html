Fractional Kelly

Mixed stock/bond portfolio with periodic rebalancing — buy low, sell high automatically

← Back to Report Buy & Hold Fractional Kelly Insurance
Fractional Kelly 8 variants  ·  2 parameters  ·  bond_frac ∈ {10%, 15%, 20%, 25%}  ×  rebalance ∈ {90, 180 days}

Hold a fixed fraction of the portfolio in bonds (cash earning the 1-year Treasury rate) and the remainder in S&P 500 equities. Rebalance back to the target allocation every 90 or 180 days. The periodic rebalance mechanically forces buying after declines and selling after rallies — a systematic contrarian tilt that can add risk-adjusted return over long horizons.

The Kelly Criterion — Background

John Kelly's 1956 formula gives the fraction of capital to wager on a favourable bet in order to maximise the long-run growth rate of the portfolio (the geometric mean). Applied to equity markets:

Full Kelly fraction (stocks)

f* = (μ − r) / σ²

where μ is the expected stock return, r is the risk-free rate, and σ² is the variance of returns.

The full Kelly bet is theoretically optimal but is highly sensitive to estimation error in μ and σ. In practice, investors use a fraction of the Kelly bet — typically 25–50% — to reduce variance and avoid catastrophic drawdowns while still capturing most of the long-run growth advantage.

In this backtest the "bond fraction" is the complement of the stock allocation: a bond_frac of 0.20 means 20% bonds, 80% stocks. A higher bond fraction corresponds to a more conservative, lower-leverage position.

How It Works

📅

Day 1

Buy (1 − bond_frac) × capital / price shares. Remaining bond_frac × capital is held as cash.

🔄

Rebalance day

Apply accrued Treasury interest to cash. Compute total portfolio value. Buy or sell shares to restore the target stock fraction.

🏁

Last day

Apply final interest to cash, sell all shares, sum cash + stock proceeds.

Rebalance mechanics (every rebalance_period days)

cash        ×= (1 + interest_rate) ^ (days_elapsed / 365)
total_value  = cash + shares × price
target_shares = (1 − bond_frac) × total_value / price
delta_shares  = target_shares − current_shares
cash        −= delta_shares × price      # positive delta → buy shares
shares      += delta_shares

The interest rate is the 1-year US Treasury yield on the rebalance date, drawn from the FRED GS1 series.

Why rebalancing adds value

After a market decline the stock allocation falls below its target. Rebalancing buys more shares at a lower price. After a rally the stock allocation grows above target; rebalancing sells some shares, locking in gains. Over many market cycles this "buy low, sell high" behaviour extracts a rebalancing bonus — a positive contribution to returns that is separate from the equity risk premium.

Parameters Explored

Parameter Values tested What it controls
bond_frac 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.25 Fraction of portfolio held in bonds/cash at each rebalance. Lower values mean more equity exposure and higher expected return with higher variance. Higher values dampen drawdowns but reduce upside.
rebalance_period 90 days, 180 days How frequently the portfolio is returned to its target allocation. Shorter periods respond faster to market moves; longer periods reduce trading frequency and are less sensitive to short-term noise.

The 4 × 2 = 8 variants span a range from Kelly 10% / 90-day (aggressive, most equity-like) to Kelly 25% / 180-day (conservative, slowest rebalance).

Parameter intuition

  • Low bond_frac (10–15%): Behaves very similarly to Buy & Hold in bull markets but captures more of the rebalancing bonus in volatile markets. Slightly higher risk at short horizons.
  • High bond_frac (20–25%): Materially lower volatility; the bond cushion reduces the fraction of losing starts at 1–3 year horizons. Ceiling on long-run returns is correspondingly lower.
  • 90-day rebalance: More reactive — captures more of the rebalancing bonus but is more sensitive to short-term whipsaws.
  • 180-day rebalance: Trades only twice a year; smoother trajectory, slightly lower rebalancing bonus but less exposure to false signals.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Systematic contrarian behaviour — no emotional decision-making
  • Bond allocation earns actual interest (FRED GS1 Treasury rate), not just sitting idle
  • Rebalancing bonus is a well-documented, robust source of return in volatile markets
  • Lower fraction of losing starts than Buy & Hold at medium horizons, especially with higher bond_frac
  • Easy to implement with annual or semi-annual tax-loss harvesting windows

Limitations

  • In a steady bull market with low volatility, rebalancing repeatedly sells the winner (equities) into bonds — underperforming Buy & Hold
  • Bond returns in this backtest track the 1-year Treasury, which has been near zero for extended periods — diluting the value of the bond allocation
  • Requires discipline to execute rebalances during drawdowns, which is the psychologically hardest moment to buy
  • Transaction costs (spread, commissions, taxes) are not modelled here; frequent rebalancing in taxable accounts can erode the bonus significantly

What the Data Shows

In the backtest results, Fractional Kelly variants typically:

  • Offer modestly lower fraction of losing starts than Buy & Hold at 5–10 year horizons, particularly with higher bond fractions
  • Converge toward — or occasionally exceed — Buy & Hold mean returns at 10–15 year horizons, especially in volatile decades
  • Show the most differentiation from Buy & Hold in periods with large mid-cycle corrections (early 2000s tech bust, 2008–09 financial crisis)
